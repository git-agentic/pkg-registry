# Namespace-ownership prior art — how ecosystems bind namespaces to organizational identity

- **Date:** 2026-07-11
- **Question:** How do existing package ecosystems bind namespaces to organizational identity, and what worked? Sentinel plans namespace ownership bound to organizational identity via DNS TXT challenge and/or Sigstore OIDC — not registry user accounts — so this extracts the mechanics it can reuse and the failure modes it must design around.
- **Resolves:** [git-agentic/pkg-registry#35](https://github.com/git-agentic/pkg-registry/issues/35) (part of #33)
- **Method:** Primary sources only where they exist — central.sonatype.org docs, PEPs at peps.python.org, PyPI blog/docs, docs.npmjs.com, the npm/attestation spec and npm CLI source, docs.sigstore.dev and sigstore/fulcio docs, go.dev/ref/mod plus the Go toolchain source and Go team blog/design docs, crates.io policies via the Rust RFCs that established them, and first-party TanStack/GitHub statements for the squatting incident. Secondary sources (Socket, Wiz, StepSecurity, etc.) are used only for corroboration and are explicitly marked. Every claim cites its source inline; exact policy language is quoted where it is load-bearing. No design recommendations — that is a later ticket.

## 1. Maven Central / Sonatype — domain-based groupId verification

Maven Central is the only major registry whose namespace (the groupId) is bound to organizational identity by design: "if you control *example.com*, you can use any groupId starting with *com.example*" ([Register a Namespace](https://central.sonatype.org/register/namespace/)). Sonatype's stated rationale is delegation of identity to DNS and code hosts: "We have historically delegated the original responsibility to DNS providers and repository hosts. Our logic is that if someone is able to act on behalf of an organization to such a degree, it is very likely that they are also authorized to publish to Maven Central" ([Why are namespaces tied to DNS?](https://central.sonatype.org/faq/namespaces-and-dns/)), plus impersonation protection: "no one wants to use fake components published by someone that's impersonating an organization such as org.apache or com.google" ([Why do I need to verify project ownership?](https://central.sonatype.org/faq/verify-ownership/)).

**DNS TXT challenge (domain groupIds).** Registering a domain-based namespace in the Central Publisher Portal issues a Verification Key tied to that registration request; the applicant publishes it as a DNS TXT record on the domain, and an automated check completes verification, typically within minutes ([Register a Namespace](https://central.sonatype.org/register/namespace/), [How do I set the TXT record?](https://central.sonatype.org/faq/how-to-set-txt-record/)). The check is domain-exact: "The automated system checks the *exact* domain for the namespace for the `TXT` record. For the `com.example` namespace, the registration process checks `example.com`. It does not check `com.example.com`, `maven-central.example.com`, or any other variation" ([FAQ](https://central.sonatype.org/faq/how-to-set-txt-record/)). The docs' terminal example shows an `OSSRH-XXYYZZ`-style key as the TXT value. The record can be removed after verification. **No URL-redirect or HTTP-file alternative challenge is documented** — DNS TXT is the sole documented method for domain namespaces (negative result across the registration and FAQ pages).

**Code-host namespaces (`io.github.username` etc.).** Two paths ([Register a Namespace](https://central.sonatype.org/register/namespace/), [verify-ownership FAQ](https://central.sonatype.org/faq/verify-ownership/)): signing up to the Portal with a GitHub account automatically grants a verified `io.github.<username>` namespace (GitHub login is itself the proof); for GitLab/Gitee/Bitbucket (or GitHub without social login), the applicant creates a temporary public repository whose *name is the Verification Key* (e.g. `gitlab.com/myusername/verification-key`), deletable after verification.

**One-time, not renewed.** Verification happens once at registration. There is no documented periodic re-check of continued domain or account control. Changes afterward are human-mediated: "If your namespace has already been verified previously, human review will be required to add users or transfer ownership" ([verify-ownership FAQ](https://central.sonatype.org/faq/verify-ownership/) — single source; the evidentiary standard for that review is not specified anywhere).

**Domain expiry / transfer: undocumented.** Sonatype publishes **no** policy on what happens when the domain behind a verified groupId expires or changes hands — no dispute-resolution document, no re-registration rule, no takeover policy was found anywhere on central.sonatype.org (confirmed silence on the pages that would carry it, including the DNS-rationale FAQ). The closest text is the trademark framing: "If a third-party were to misrepresent themselves as an organization and if we did NOT have these controls, the authoritative organization could submit a trademark/copyright violation notice to us asking for the removal of components published without valid organizational permission" ([namespaces-and-dns FAQ](https://central.sonatype.org/faq/namespaces-and-dns/)) — i.e., post-hoc takedown on IP grounds, not a namespace-lifecycle policy. Observed failure mode, stated plainly: a one-shot DNS check with no renewal and no expiry policy means whoever controls the domain *at claim time* holds the namespace indefinitely, and the registry has no documented answer for the domain's next owner.

**Legacy grandfathering.** Pre-Portal groupIds were claimed via human-reviewed JIRA tickets at issues.sonatype.org; that instance is decommissioned and the ticket-level verification steps are no longer retrievable ([What happened to issues.sonatype.org?](https://central.sonatype.org/faq/what-happened-to-issues-sonatype-org/)). "As of June 30, 2025 OSSRH has reached end of life... All OSSRH namespaces have been migrated to Central Publisher Portal" ([OSSRH Sunset](https://central.sonatype.org/pages/ossrh-eol/)) — whether migration entailed fresh re-verification is not stated (confirmed silence). Genuinely legacy non-domain groupIds (short names from Maven's early years) predate all of this and were carried along.

| Mechanic | Maven Central |
|---|---|
| Claim | Namespace request in Central Portal → Sonatype-issued Verification Key |
| Challenge | DNS TXT record with the key on the *exact* domain; or temp public repo named after the key on the code host; `io.github.*` auto-granted on GitHub login |
| Renewal | None documented — one-time at registration |
| Transfer / dispute | "Human review" for adding users / transferring verified namespaces (standard unspecified); trademark takedown route; no domain-expiry policy at all |
| Grandfathering | OSSRH-era namespaces migrated wholesale June 2025; re-verification unstated |

## 2. PyPI — PEP 541 disputes, organizations, PEP 752 namespaces

**PEP 541 (Status: Final, Type: Process — [peps.python.org/pep-0541](https://peps.python.org/pep-0541/))** governs name disputes on a first-come-first-served index. Its categories:

- **Invalid** projects ("A project published on the Package Index meeting ANY of the following is considered invalid and will be removed from the Index") include the squatting criterion: "project is name squatting (package has no functionality or is empty)".
- **Abandoned**: "A project is considered *abandoned* when ALL of the following are met: owner not reachable (see Reachability above); no releases within the past twelve months; and no activity from the owner on the project's home page (or no home page listed)." Abandoned projects can be transferred to new maintainers under further conditions (candidate demonstrates failed contact attempts, provides evidence of continuation).
- **Reachability**: "The user of the Package Index is solely responsible for being reachable by the Package Index maintainers for matters concerning projects that the user owns... The maintainers stop trying to reach the user after six weeks" — a six-week contact window is the operative timeline.
- **Adjudication**: PyPI maintainers "are not arbiters in disputes involving active projects"; the **Packaging Workgroup** has "the final say in any disputes covered by this document and can decide to reassign or remove a project from the Package Index after careful consideration." Requests are filed as issues on [github.com/pypa/pypi-support](https://github.com/pypa/pypi-support/issues).
- Remedies: transfer to a new owner, or removal from the index.

Operational reality: the pypi-support tracker carries a persistent multi-hundred-issue PEP 541 backlog. That characterization is corroborated *by a peer registry's primary source*: Rust's RFC 3646 cites it as precedent — "PyPI has policies under PEP 541 and they are not able to keep up with the requests" ([RFC 3646](https://rust-lang.github.io/rfcs/3646-remove-crate-transfer-mediation-policy.html)). Treat the backlog's exact size as unquantified here.

**Organization accounts.** "Organizations on PyPI are self-managed teams, with their own exclusive branded web addresses" ([Introducing PyPI Organizations, 2023-04-23](https://blog.pypi.org/posts/2023-04-23-introducing-pypi-organizations/)); "available to community projects for free, forever, and to corporate projects for a small fee," with revenue funding PyPI. Requests undergo manual review/approval by PyPI admins, but **neither the announcement nor [docs.pypi.org/organization-accounts](https://docs.pypi.org/organization-accounts/) documents any verification of real-world organizational identity** — no domain proof, no legal-entity check is described (confirmed silence). An organization is an account-model construct: teams, permissions, "a unique brand," not verified identity, and it reserves no package-name prefix by itself.

**PEP 752 — "Implicit namespaces for package repositories" ([peps.python.org/pep-0752](https://peps.python.org/pep-0752/)). Status: Accepted (Standards Track), Resolution 29-Jun-2026.** Mechanics: a namespace grant to an (organization) owner covers "a project that exactly matches the namespace itself" and "projects that start with the namespace followed by a hyphen" — the NuGet-style *reserved-prefix-over-a-flat-namespace* model, explicitly not npm-style scoped syntax. Grants must not overlap (if `foo-bar` exists as a grant, `foo` can only go to the same owner); names are normalized per packaging rules; repositories "SHOULD impose a depth limit on the number of hyphens in a namespace." Granting policy is delegated: "Policies for granting and managing namespaces are not discussed here as they are specific to each index." The **grandfathering rule is a SHOULD, not a MUST**: "Repositories SHOULD have an exception to this rule for projects that existed before the namespace was reserved" — pre-existing projects under a newly reserved prefix keep working, but the PEP does not mandate it. PEP 755 ("Implicit namespace policy for PyPI") is the related PyPI-specific policy proposal.

**Trusted Publishers** ([docs.pypi.org/trusted-publishers](https://docs.pypi.org/trusted-publishers/)) bind an OIDC workload identity (CI service + user/org + repository, e.g. GitHub Actions) to *per-project publish authorization* — "Projects on PyPI can be configured to trust a particular configuration on a particular CI service." It is publish auth, not namespace ownership. The "pending publisher" variant lets a user pre-configure a publisher for a project name that doesn't exist yet, but "A 'pending' publisher does **not** create a project or reserve a project's name **until** it is actually used to publish" — if someone else registers the name first, the pending publisher is simply invalidated ([creating-a-project-through-oidc](https://docs.pypi.org/trusted-publishers/creating-a-project-through-oidc/)).

| Mechanic | PyPI |
|---|---|
| Claim | First-come-first-served project names; org accounts manually approved (identity not verified); PEP 752 prefix grants via organizations (index-specific policy) |
| Challenge | None at registration; disputes are post-hoc under PEP 541 |
| Adjudicator | Packaging Workgroup (final say); filed via pypi-support tracker |
| Timeline | Six-week reachability window before a maintainer is deemed unreachable; abandoned = unreachable + 12 months without releases + no home-page activity |
| Grandfathering | PEP 752: pre-existing projects under a reserved prefix "SHOULD" be excepted |

## 3. npm + Sigstore — what OIDC identity actually binds

npm provenance is **two structurally different attestations** ([Generating provenance statements](https://docs.npmjs.com/generating-provenance-statements); [npm/attestation publish spec v0.1](https://github.com/npm/attestation/tree/main/specs/publish/v0.1)):

- The **publish attestation** is generated by the registry and carries, in its predicate, only `name`, `version`, `registry`, with the subject digest being the SHA-512 of the tarball. No repository, commit, or workflow field exists in it — it attests "an authenticated npm account published this," i.e. it is bound to the *account* model.
- The **provenance attestation** is a SLSA-format statement signed via Sigstore, generated by the npm CLI when building on a supported cloud CI (GitHub Actions or GitLab CI/CD, cloud-hosted runners only). For GitHub Actions the CLI populates ([npm/cli libnpmpublish/lib/provenance.js](https://github.com/npm/cli/blob/latest/workspaces/libnpmpublish/lib/provenance.js)): `buildType` = the GitHub Actions workflow SLSA buildType, `externalParameters.workflow.{ref,repository,path}`, `resolvedDependencies[0]` = `git+<server>/<repo>@<ref>` with `digest.gitCommit = GITHUB_SHA`, `builder.id` naming the runner environment. Attestations are logged to the public Sigstore transparency log (Rekor).

The **Fulcio certificate** signing that attestation independently carries the same identity ([oid-info.md](https://github.com/sigstore/fulcio/blob/main/docs/oid-info.md), [OIDC in Fulcio](https://docs.sigstore.dev/certificate_authority/oidc-in-fulcio/)): SAN = `https://github.com/{job_workflow_ref}` for GitHub Actions workload identity (GitLab uses `ci_config_ref_uri`); OID extensions carry the OIDC issuer (1.3.6.1.4.1.57264.1.8, Issuer V2), Build Signer URI (.1.9), Source Repository URI (.1.12), Token Subject (.1.24), plus deprecated GitHub-specific OIDs for event name/sha/workflow/repository/ref (.1.2–.1.6). Email-based identities require `email_verified: true` from the IdP. "Fulcio signs X.509 certificates valid for 10 minutes" ([Certificate Authority overview](https://docs.sigstore.dev/certificate_authority/overview/)); after-the-fact verification therefore leans on Rekor inclusion or RFC 3161 signed timestamps.

**Trusted publishing** ([docs.npmjs.com/trusted-publishers](https://docs.npmjs.com/trusted-publishers)) exchanges a CI OIDC token for a short-lived publish credential, configured *per package*: provider org/user + repo, exact workflow filename (case-sensitive), optional environment, allowed action. GA July 31, 2025 ([GitHub changelog](https://github.blog/changelog/2025-07-31-npm-trusted-publishing-with-oidc-is-generally-available/)) with GitHub Actions and GitLab CI/CD; the current docs list a third provider (CircleCI cloud) — presumably added post-GA, but no dated announcement was found (open discrepancy). Configuration happens from the settings of a package you already control; nothing in the docs makes trusted publishing a path to *claiming* a name or scope.

**What the OIDC identity proves — and doesn't.** It proves build origin: workflow W in repository R at commit C under issuer I produced these bytes. It does **not** prove namespace legitimacy: no field in the Fulcio cert, the SLSA predicate, or the publish attestation references the npm scope/account, and no npm mechanism links a scope to a GitHub org or domain. npm scopes are pure account artifacts: an org named `wombat` automatically gets `@wombat` ([About organization scopes and packages](https://docs.npmjs.com/about-organization-scopes-and-packages)); user scopes come from `npm init --scope=@username` after signup ([Creating and publishing scoped public packages](https://docs.npmjs.com/creating-and-publishing-scoped-public-packages)). No verification step ties either to any external identity (documented silence on both sides). So the GitHub/OIDC identity plane and the npm account/namespace plane are disjoint registries with no field-level linkage.

**Failure mode, demonstrated:** a compromised workflow signs valid provenance for malicious code, because the identity check confirms *which workflow ran*, not that its logic wasn't subverted. First-party in-ecosystem example: TanStack's May 2026 compromise worked by "in-memory OIDC token extraction" from a poisoned CI pipeline — no npm credential was stolen ([TanStack postmortem](https://tanstack.com/blog/npm-supply-chain-compromise-postmortem), see §6). Cross-ecosystem corroboration: PyPI's first-party analysis of the ultralytics attack, where a `pull_request_target` injection made the legitimate workflow publish malware — and where a later wave that bypassed CI was detectable precisely by its *missing* attestation ([PyPI blog, 2024-12-11](https://blog.pypi.org/posts/2024-12-11-ultralytics-attack-analysis/)). Attestation is a detection signal, not a prevention.

| Claim | Where bound |
|---|---|
| OIDC issuer | Fulcio cert OID 1.3.6.1.4.1.57264.1.8 |
| Workflow that ran | Fulcio cert SAN `https://github.com/{job_workflow_ref}` |
| Source repo / commit | Cert OIDs .1.12/.1.3 (deprec.) and SLSA `externalParameters` / `resolvedDependencies[0].digest.gitCommit` |
| Runner environment | SLSA `builder.id` |
| Package name/version | Registry publish attestation predicate only |
| npm scope ownership | npm account/org system only — **no attestation or cert field** |
| GitHub-org ↔ npm-scope link | **Does not exist in any primary source** |

## 4. Go modules — namespace = continuous domain control

Go has no registry accounts at all: "there is no such thing as a package repository account. The import path of a package embeds the information that `go mod download` needs in order to fetch its module directly from the VCS" ([How Go Mitigates Supply Chain Attacks](https://go.dev/blog/supply-chain)). Module paths are domain-rooted by rule: "The leading path element (up to the first slash, if any), by convention a domain name, must contain only lower-case ASCII letters, ASCII digits, dots... it must contain at least one dot and cannot start with a dash" ([go.dev/ref/mod](https://go.dev/ref/mod#module-path)).

**The go-import challenge.** For a custom (vanity) path, the toolchain requests `https://<module-path>?go-get=1` and parses `<meta name="go-import" content="prefix vcs reporoot [subdir]">` from the response ([go.dev/ref/mod#vcs-find](https://go.dev/ref/mod#vcs-find); implementation in [cmd/go/internal/vcs/vcs.go](https://github.com/golang/go/blob/master/src/cmd/go/internal/vcs/vcs.go), whose comment documents "the parsed `<meta name="go-import" content="prefix vcs reporoot subdir" />` tags"; the pseudo-vcs `mod` value redirects resolution to a proxy instead of a VCS). Namespace ownership is therefore *the ability to serve HTTPS content on the domain*, proven implicitly at every fresh resolution — no registration, no one-shot ceremony.

**Continuity and its limits.** The check runs whenever an unresolved path is directly fetched, so control is effectively continuous for *new* resolutions — but the ecosystem's caching layers deliberately decouple *published history* from the domain:

- The module mirror "caches metadata and source code in its own storage system, allowing the mirror to continue to serve source code that is no longer available from the original locations... protect you from disappearing dependencies" ([Module Mirror and Checksum Database Launched](https://go.dev/blog/module-mirror-launch)).
- The checksum database pins every observed `(module, version)`: "Even the author of a module can't move their tags around or otherwise change the bits associated with a specific version from one day to the next without the change being detected" ([ibid.](https://go.dev/blog/module-mirror-launch)). But the guarantee is explicitly consistency, not authenticity: "'correct' for a particular module version download is defined as the same code everyone else downloads" ([sumdb design proposal](https://go.googlesource.com/proposal/+/master/design/25530-sumdb.md)).

**When a domain changes hands:** the new owner controls what fresh direct fetches resolve, and can publish never-before-seen version strings whose first observation the sumdb will happily record as canonical. Existing versions are immune (proxy keeps serving them; any byte change is detected and refused), new versions are the new owner's to mint. The Go team's own writing does not treat domain-ownership transfer as a designed-for threat: the one first-party discussion of vanity-import fragility ([golang/go#26160](https://github.com/golang/go/issues/26160) and its [DNS-based vanity imports design doc](https://go.googlesource.com/proposal/+/master/design/26160-dns-based-vanity-imports.md)) is scoped to "expired certificates, problems with bespoke servers, timeouts" — server reliability, not hostile takeover (confirmed silence in both documents). Enforcement caveat: CVE-2026-42501 / [GO-2026-4984](https://osv.dev/vulnerability/GO-2026-4984) documents a `cmd/go` bug that treated a no-entry sumdb response as validation success, letting a malicious proxy serve unvalidated content until patched — a gap in enforcing the guarantee, not in its design.

| Mechanic | Go modules |
|---|---|
| Claim | Serve the `go-import` meta tag (or VCS) at `https://<path>?go-get=1` |
| Challenge | Implicit, at every fresh direct resolution — continuous domain control, no registration |
| Renewal | Continuous for new fetches; never re-checked for versions already observed |
| Failure containment | Proxy cache survives origin loss; sumdb freezes observed versions ("consistency, not authenticity"); **no protection for new versions after domain transfer** |

## 5. crates.io — explicit refusal to verify or adjudicate

crates.io is first-come-first-served by policy, codified in 2023 by [RFC 3463](https://rust-lang.github.io/rfcs/3463-crates-io-policy-update.html) (announced on the [Rust blog, 2023-09-22](https://blog.rust-lang.org/2023/09/22/crates-io-usage-policy-rfc.html); live at [crates.io/policies](https://crates.io/policies)), explicitly modeled "based on prior work by PyPI, npm and GitHub": "crates.io has a first-come, first-serve policy on crate names"; "Upon publishing a package, the publisher will be made owner of the package." Squatting is defined — a crate that "exists only to reserve a name for a prolonged period of time (often called 'name squatting') without having any genuine functionality, purpose, or significant development activity" — and enforced only reactively ("The crates.io team may delete crates from the registry that do not comply with the policies... in most cases the team will first give the author the chance to justify the purpose of the crate"). Disputes are discretionary: "We will interpret our policies and resolve disputes in favor of protecting users as a whole. The crates.io team reserves the possibility to evaluate each instance on a case-by-case basis."

**The refusal hardened in 2024.** RFC 3463 still offered soft mediation for unreachable owners; [RFC 3646 "Remove Crate Transfer Mediation Policy"](https://rust-lang.github.io/rfcs/3646-remove-crate-transfer-mediation-policy.html) (merged 2024-06-24) deleted it. Current policy: "If someone wants a crate name that is currently in use, and their efforts to either find contact information for or get a response from the current owner have been unsuccessful, they will need to pick a different name for their crate. **Any requests to the crates.io team to mediate will be declined.**" The stated reasons are the most explicit adjudication-cost testimony in any registry's primary record: (a) "Managing these requests take time, and they aren't even usually successful. The crates.io team would like to spend their time working on the site rather than providing this crate mediation service" (maintainer carols10cents, [RFC PR #3646](https://github.com/rust-lang/rfcs/pull/3646)); (b) peer precedent — "PyPI has policies under PEP 541 and they are not able to keep up with the requests," and npm's process was "'not available for dispute requests due to lack of activity'"; (c) a security argument from the PR discussion: official mediation "carries more social weight than 'random stranger on the Internet asks for transfer'" — i.e., the mediation channel itself becomes a social-engineering vector for hostile takeovers. Voluntary transfer remains: the current owner adds the requester as owner and removes themselves.

There is no general reserved-names/trademark list — only a technical blocklist of Windows device names (after a crate named `nul` broke Cargo on Windows in 2017), and the Rust project's own convention of publishing "an empty placeholder crate" to hold official names ([Rust Forge crate-ownership policy](https://forge.rust-lang.org/policies/crate-ownership.html) — Rust-project-internal, not platform policy; that page also shows name-follows-project on maintainer turnover: "the `crates.io` name is kept by the Rust project and the new group of maintainers will need to pick a new crate name").

**[RFC 3243 "Packages as (optional) namespaces"](https://rust-lang.github.io/rfcs/3243-packages-as-optional-namespaces.html)** — merged 2024-03-11, **not yet shipped end-to-end** (2025H1 project-goals status: "Cargo support is partially implemented. No compiler support"; crates.io tracking issue [rust-lang/crates.io#8292](https://github.com/rust-lang/crates.io/issues/8292) still open at last check). Mechanics: `parent::child` crate names where "all owners of foo are always considered owners of foo::bar." Verification scope is deliberately minimal: ownership of the parent *crate name* — itself first-come-first-served — with no organizational identity anywhere. Trusted Publishing (announced [2025-07-11](https://blog.rust-lang.org/2025/07/11/crates-io-development-update-2025-07/), designed in [RFC 3691](https://rust-lang.github.io/rfcs/3691-trusted-publishing-cratesio.html)) is OIDC publish-auth for an already-owned crate ("A Trusted Publisher Configuration can only be created after an initial manual publishing of a crate") — not namespace identity.

| Mechanic | crates.io |
|---|---|
| Claim | First publish = ownership; no identity check of any kind |
| Challenge | None |
| Dispute | Reactive squatting takedowns at team discretion; **mediation categorically refused** (RFC 3646); voluntary transfer only |
| Namespaces | RFC 3243 `parent::child` merged, unshipped; gates on parent-crate ownership only |

## 6. The TanStack-class npm squatting incidents (2025–2026)

Three separate events are commonly conflated; they fail in three *different* places of npm's model. (Chronology assembled from first-party sources where available; vendor writeups marked secondary.)

**(a) September/November 2025 — Shai-Hulud worm.** Self-propagating malware spreading via *compromised maintainer accounts and tokens* on legitimate packages — no lookalike names involved (corroborated: [CISA alert 2025-09-23](https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/alerts/2025/09/23/widespread-supply-chain-compromise-impacting-npm-ecosystem); Krebs on Security, secondary). GitHub's response ([Our plan for a more secure npm supply chain](https://github.blog/security/supply-chain-security/our-plan-for-a-more-secure-npm-supply-chain/)) was authentication-only — mandatory 2FA directions, 7-day granular tokens, trusted publishing, classic-token deprecation — and contains **nothing** about naming policy (confirmed by direct read). Relevant here only as context and as the trigger for npm's auth hardening.

**(b) April 29, 2026 — the unscoped `tanstack` package: the actual namespace failure.** The unscoped npm name `tanstack` — distinct from the legitimate `@tanstack/*` scope — was held by an unaffiliated user ("sh20raj"), and shipped malicious versions 2.0.4–2.0.7 in a ~27-minute window, whose postinstall scripts exfiltrated `.env`/`.env.local`/`.env.production` to a dead-drop endpoint (**secondary sources**: [Socket](https://socket.dev/blog/tanstack-brandsquat-compromise), independently corroborated by [securityonline.info/Aikido](https://securityonline.info/npm-tanstack-typosquatting-supply-chain-attack-env-theft/)). First-party evidence that this was a long-standing unresolved squat: Tanner Linsley's pre-incident public post to the holder — "we could really use the `tanstack` npm package name. We've proactively reached out via email many times in the past with no response but are now getting complaints from unsuspecting users and agents mistaking it for the official TanStack CLI" ([@tannerlinsley on X](https://x.com/tannerlinsley/status/2032196428521685047); wording captured via search snippet, live page not re-fetchable — fidelity caveat). Socket additionally reports (single-source, relayed) that TanStack filed a trademark claim, that the holder had earlier demanded $10,000 for the name, and that repeated appeals to npm went nowhere. No first-party TanStack blog post about this April incident, and no npm/GitHub statement on it, was found (genuine gap).

**Why npm's model permitted it:** scopes protect only the exact scope string, which is an account artifact ([About scopes](https://docs.npmjs.com/about-scopes)); the unscoped namespace remains first-come-first-served with no reservation or trademark screening ([Creating and publishing unscoped public packages](https://docs.npmjs.com/creating-and-publishing-unscoped-public-packages)); and the disputes policy is post-hoc and deliberately non-adjudicative — reserving names "simply for the purposes of reserving it for future use" violates the Terms of Use and soliciting payment for a name is sanctionable, but "**npm does not resolve squatting claims on demand**," and trademark complaints route through GitHub's formal Trademark Policy process, "the only path npm acts upon for intellectual property disputes" ([Package Name Disputes](https://docs.npmjs.com/policies/disputes)). Owning `@tanstack` gave TanStack nothing over `tanstack`, `tanstack-*`, or lookalike scopes.

**(c) May 11–12, 2026 — the `@tanstack/*` CI compromise ("Mini Shai-Hulud" / TeamPCP).** Attackers chained a `pull_request_target` vulnerability, GitHub Actions cache poisoning, and in-memory OIDC token extraction to publish 84 malicious versions across 42 `@tanstack/*` packages in ~6 minutes, self-propagating by republishing other maintainers' packages; "No maintainer was phished, had a password leak, or a token stolen from their account" (**first-party**: [TanStack postmortem](https://tanstack.com/blog/npm-supply-chain-compromise-postmortem), [hardening follow-up](https://tanstack.com/blog/incident-followup), [TanStack/router#7383](https://github.com/TanStack/router/issues/7383); detection credited to StepSecurity, secondary, with Wiz/Orca/Snyk corroboration — campaign-wide counts conflict across vendors, 150+ vs 160+ packages including UiPath and others). Wiz states explicitly this was "legitimate organization compromise, not typosquatting or lookalike domains" (secondary). Relevance: this is the OIDC/CI failure mode of §3 happening to the *same org* one month after the squatting failure — the two attacks breached different layers, and neither layer's defense helped the other. TanStack's remediation was CI hardening (SHA-pinned actions, `pull_request_target` removal, cache disabling, FIDO 2FA); no npm naming-policy change resulted from either incident (negative result across npm/GitHub first-party channels).

## 7. Cross-cutting extraction

### 7.1 Claim/challenge mechanics compared

| Model | Exemplar | What is proven | When | Failure modes observed/documented |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **One-shot DNS TXT** | Maven Central | Control of the exact apex domain at claim time | Once, at registration; never renewed | Domain expiry/transfer leaves a permanently verified namespace with no documented reclamation or dispute path (§1 — confirmed policy silence). DNS takeover during the challenge window ⇒ valid claim. Verification key + automated check is cheap and fast (minutes) — that part demonstrably works at ecosystem scale |
| **One-shot repo challenge** | Maven Central (code hosts) | Control of a code-host account at claim time | Once | Same staleness problem plus host-side username recycling; `io.github.*` auto-grant collapses the namespace onto the host's account model |
| **Continuous domain control** | Go modules | Ability to serve HTTPS content on the domain, at every fresh resolution | Continuous (per unresolved fetch) | Domain's *new* owner inherits the namespace for new versions immediately and silently; mitigated for history only — proxy cache + sumdb freeze already-observed versions ("consistency, not authenticity", §4). Availability coupling: dead domain ⇒ new consumers can't resolve without the proxy cache. Server/cert fragility acknowledged first-party ([golang/go#26160](https://github.com/golang/go/issues/26160)) |
| **OIDC workload identity** | npm/Sigstore, PyPI & crates.io trusted publishing | That workflow W in repo R at commit C under issuer I built/published the artifact — 10-minute certs, transparency-logged | Per publish | Proves build origin, **not namespace legitimacy** — no field links the CI identity to the registry namespace (§3). CI compromise yields *valid* attestations for malicious code (TanStack May 2026, first-party; ultralytics, first-party PyPI). Issuer compromise inherits everything downstream. Absence-of-attestation is a useful detection signal (ultralytics wave 2) |
| **Account-based (baseline)** | npm scopes, PyPI orgs, crates.io | Control of a registry account with a matching name | At account creation | No tie to any external identity; brand-adjacent names outside the exact scope/prefix remain open (tanstack April 2026); org-name squatting is itself possible; adjudication backlogs (PEP 541) or refusal (crates.io, npm) leave victims without remedy |

Two mechanics compose visibly in the wild: Maven's *identity at claim time* and Go's *content immutability after observation* solve different halves; npm/Sigstore adds *per-artifact build identity* orthogonal to both. No surveyed ecosystem binds all three, and none re-checks organizational identity after the initial claim except Go's (incidental, availability-coupled) continuous check.

### 7.2 Transfer & dispute precedents

- **PEP 541 (adjudicated):** defined categories (invalid/abandoned/squatting), a named adjudicator with final say (Packaging Workgroup), a concrete evidence test (six-week reachability, 12-month inactivity, documented contact attempts), and remedies (transfer/removal). Cost in practice: a backlog its peers cite as a cautionary tale (per RFC 3646, crates.io's primary record).
- **crates.io (refusal):** categorically declines mediation (RFC 3646), for stated reasons — time cost, low success rate, and the insight that an official mediation channel is itself a social-engineering attack surface. Accepts only voluntary owner-initiated transfer; retains discretionary reactive removal for policy-violating squats.
- **npm (refusal + IP escape hatch):** "npm does not resolve squatting claims on demand"; pay-for-name demands are sanctionable; the only adjudicated path is GitHub's formal trademark process. The April 2026 `tanstack` case shows the practical result: years of unanswered outreach, a $10k demand (secondary-sourced), then malware — resolved by takedown only after the payload shipped.
- **Maven (domain-follows-owner + human review):** the namespace tracks whoever proved domain control at claim time; subsequent user-additions/transfers require unspecified "human review"; trademark notice is the documented recourse against impersonation. No expiry/transfer lifecycle exists on paper.
- **Evidence accepted, compared:** PEP 541 accepts *conduct* evidence (unreachability, inactivity); Maven accepts *technical control* evidence (DNS/repo challenge) and, implicitly, legal evidence (trademark notices); npm accepts only legal evidence (trademark); crates.io accepts none.

### 7.3 Grandfathering a verified layer onto an unverified corpus

The exact problem Sentinel faces — every surveyed overlay chose coexistence over migration:

- **PEP 752** is the only *specified* rule set: prefix grants overlay the existing flat index, with "Repositories SHOULD have an exception to this rule for projects that existed before the namespace was reserved" — legacy projects under a newly reserved prefix survive, non-mandatorily, and the grant-overlap rule (`foo` only grantable to `foo-bar`'s owner if `foo-bar` exists) prevents a new grant from enclosing someone else's existing project. Consumer-visible trust signaling (which packages under a prefix are the grantee's vs grandfathered) is delegated to index UI.
- **Maven** grandfathered twice: early non-domain groupIds persist un-verified alongside verified ones, and the entire OSSRH corpus was migrated wholesale into the Portal in June 2025 with no stated re-verification — verified and legacy namespaces are indistinguishable in the coordinate system itself.
- **npm** overlaid scopes (2014-era) on the unscoped corpus and never connected them: `@tanstack` and `tanstack` are unrelated ownership-wise. The April 2026 incident is the canonical demonstration that *an overlay which leaves brand-equivalent legacy names claimable transfers the risk to exactly those names*.
- **Go** grandfathers implicitly and mechanically: everything already observed by proxy+sumdb stays immutable and fetchable regardless of later ownership changes — history is safe by construction, while the *identity* layer (domain control) silently follows the domain.
- **crates.io** declined the overlay entirely so far (RFC 3243 merged but unshipped, and when shipped will gate only on parent-crate account ownership).
